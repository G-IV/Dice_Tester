Die Tester — Optical Path

Folded camera → mirror → tower

How the system works

The camera is mounted horizontally, pointing along the X axis toward a 45° first-surface mirror sitting at the origin. The mirror folds the optical path 90° upward into a vertical cylindrical tower. A die resting at the base of the tower is photographed from below, through the mirror, without the camera needing a clear vertical line of sight.

Why not just centre everything?

The instinctive layout — camera optical axis level with the mirror centre, tower centred directly above — turns out not to be optimal. Three geometric effects make deliberate offsets necessary.

1. The FOV footprint on the mirror is a trapezoid

Corner rays aimed slightly upward travel further before striking the 45° mirror plane (y = x) than corner rays aimed slightly downward. The footprint is therefore wider at the top of the mirror and narrower at the bottom — a trapezoid, not a rectangle. This asymmetry is baked into the geometry and exists even when the camera is perfectly centred.

2. Camera Y offset (−14 mm)

With the camera at Y = 0 the reflected optical axis goes straight up through the mirror centre. But the trapezoidal distortion means the centroid of the four reflected FOV corners is not at the same point as the optical axis. Dropping the camera 14 mm below the mirror centre shifts the footprint so that after reflection the image is positioned symmetrically over the 3.75″ capture disc at the tower base.

3. Tower X offset (−5 mm)

Once the camera is offset in Y, its optical axis hits the mirror at (y, y, 0) where y = camY, and the reflected axis emerges at that same X coordinate — not at X = 0. The spread of the four reflected corner rays around that axis is also asymmetric. Empirically, placing the tower centre 5 mm to the left aligns the 3.75″ capture ring within the reflected FOV with the smallest margin wasted on either side.

4. Horizontal leg & mirror fill (116 mm)

Camera distance controls resolution vs. coverage. Too close: the FOV extends past the mirror edges, wasting pixels on the mirror frame. Too far: the mirror underfills the FOV, reducing resolution on the die. At 116 mm the top and bottom edges of the vertical FOV align exactly with the top and bottom of the mirror face — every pixel captured is useful reflected image.

5. Tower bottom Y (139 mm)

The tower must clear the mirror’s highest point (~42 mm) with a safety margin. At 139 mm the die entrance sits high enough to avoid collision while still being well within the reflected FOV cone.
